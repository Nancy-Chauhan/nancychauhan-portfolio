2022 was a year of growth, visibility, and preparation. I spoke at my first major conference in India, became part of the Women Tech Network, deepened my work at Gitpod, and continued building my presence in the open source community. It was also the year I made the decision to pursue graduate school — a choice that would shape everything that followed. Here is the story of how 2022 unfolded.

## DevOpsDays India

The highlight of my year was speaking at DevOpsDays India. It was my first time presenting at a large, in-person conference, and the experience was both nerve-wracking and exhilarating.

My talk focused on "Building Developer-Friendly Observability Pipelines" — a topic born from my experience at Gitpod, where we dealt with the challenge of making observability accessible to developers who are not infrastructure specialists. The core argument was that observability tools are often designed by and for operations teams, leaving developers to wrestle with unfamiliar interfaces, cryptic query languages, and dashboards that answer questions they did not ask.

I walked through practical patterns for building observability into the development workflow: auto-instrumentation, opinionated default dashboards, contextual alerts that link directly to relevant code, and lightweight local development setups that let developers experiment with tracing and metrics before deploying to production.

The response was encouraging. Several audience members came up afterward to share their own struggles with developer adoption of observability tools, and the conversations continued over coffee and chai for the rest of the day. It validated something I had been feeling — that the gap between infrastructure tooling and developer experience is a problem worth solving.

DevOpsDays also connected me with the broader Indian DevOps community, which is vibrant, warm, and growing rapidly. I met engineers working on fascinating problems at companies of all sizes, from early-stage startups to large enterprises. The Indian tech community's energy is infectious, and I left the conference with a full notebook and a long list of new connections.

## Women Tech Network

In 2022, I joined the Women Tech Network, a global community supporting women in technology through mentorship, networking, and skill development. My involvement started with attending events and quickly evolved into active participation — I mentored junior engineers, participated in panel discussions on career development in open source, and contributed to content around cloud native technologies.

The Women Tech Network gave me something I had been missing: a peer group of women navigating similar challenges in tech. The conversations were honest — about imposter syndrome, about being the only woman in a meeting, about negotiating compensation, about the invisible labor of being a "diversity representative." Having a space to discuss these things openly, without judgment, was grounding.

I also realized through this network that my own journey — from open source contributor to conference speaker to developer advocate — could serve as a roadmap for others. Several women I mentored told me that seeing someone with a similar background succeed in the cloud native space made it feel achievable for them. That kind of impact is deeply motivating.

## Gitpod

2022 was a significant year in my work at Gitpod, the cloud development environment platform. Gitpod provides automated, ready-to-code development environments that spin up in seconds, eliminating the "works on my machine" problem that plagues every engineering team.

My work at Gitpod spanned several areas:

**Infrastructure and observability.** I worked on the monitoring stack that gives Gitpod's engineering team visibility into workspace performance, IDE responsiveness, and infrastructure health. This involved Prometheus, Grafana, and custom exporters — skills I had developed through open source contributions and now applied at production scale.

**Developer experience improvements.** I contributed to making Gitpod easier to use for new developers, including improving documentation, streamlining the onboarding flow, and building example configurations for popular frameworks and languages.

**Open source advocacy.** Gitpod is open source, and part of my role involved engaging with the community — reviewing contributions, triaging issues, and helping external contributors navigate the codebase. The Gitpod community is one of the most active I have been part of, with contributors from around the world improving the platform daily.

Working at Gitpod deepened my conviction that developer experience is not a luxury — it is a multiplier. Every minute saved in environment setup, every friction point removed from the development workflow, compounds across an entire engineering organization. The tools we build for developers have an outsized impact on productivity and, ultimately, on the software those developers produce.

## Open Source Contributions

Outside of Gitpod, I continued contributing to the broader open source ecosystem in 2022:

**Prometheus ecosystem.** I maintained my contributions to Prometheus-related projects, including improvements to client libraries and exporter documentation. The observability space continued to evolve rapidly, with OpenTelemetry gaining significant traction and the lines between metrics, traces, and logs blurring.

**CNCF projects.** I participated in CNCF Special Interest Groups and contributed to discussions around improving the contributor experience across CNCF projects. The CNCF's governance model — open, transparent, and community-driven — continues to be a source of inspiration.

**Hacktoberfest.** I participated as both a contributor and a maintainer during Hacktoberfest 2022. As a maintainer, I tagged issues as "good first issue" and spent time reviewing and mentoring first-time contributors. The experience reinforced how important it is to invest in making projects approachable.

**Blog posts and talks.** I wrote technical blog posts about Prometheus exporters, Docker optimization, and cloud native development workflows. Writing remains one of the best ways I know to solidify my own understanding and share it with others.

## The Graduate School Decision

The most consequential decision of 2022 was choosing to apply to graduate school. It was not an obvious choice — I had a fulfilling career, a growing presence in the community, and no shortage of interesting work. Why go back to school?

The honest answer is that I wanted depth. My industry experience gave me breadth — I had worked across infrastructure, observability, developer tooling, and advocacy. But I felt gaps in my theoretical foundations. I wanted to understand distributed systems not just as a practitioner but as someone who could reason formally about correctness, consistency, and fault tolerance. I wanted to explore the intersection of machine learning and infrastructure. I wanted the intellectual rigor of a research environment.

I applied to several programs and was accepted at Cornell University. The acceptance letter arrived on a Tuesday afternoon, and I sat staring at it for a long time, feeling a mix of excitement and anxiety. It would mean leaving my job, my community in India, and everything familiar. But it also felt like the next right step.

## Personal Reflections

2022 taught me a few things:

**Visibility matters.** Speaking at DevOpsDays, writing blog posts, and being active in communities opened doors I did not know existed. If you are doing interesting work, share it. The right people will notice.

**Mentorship is bidirectional.** Every person I mentored taught me something — about their experiences, their challenges, their perspective. Mentorship is not a one-way transfer of knowledge; it is a conversation that enriches both sides.

**Big decisions deserve time.** The graduate school decision took months of deliberation, conversations with mentors, and honest self-reflection. Rushing a life-changing decision serves no one. Take the time you need.

**Community is a career strategy.** My involvement in open source and the CNCF ecosystem was not strategic in the beginning — it was driven by genuine interest. But looking back, the relationships, skills, and reputation I built through community work have been the most valuable investments of my career.

## Looking Ahead

As I write this, I am preparing for the biggest transition of my life — moving to the United States to start at Cornell in the fall of 2023. The coming year will bring new challenges, new opportunities, and an enormous amount of change. I am ready for it.

Thank you, 2022. You gave me a stage, a network, a purpose, and the courage to make a leap. Let us see where 2023 takes us.
